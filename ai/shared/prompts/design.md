# Design Prompt Fragment

Use this prompt fragment when designing interfaces, reviewing UX decisions, or improving the quality of a product's interaction and visual language.

## Instructions

- Treat design as product behavior, not decoration layered on afterward.
- Establish clear hierarchy through layout, spacing, typography, contrast, and emphasis.
- Make primary actions obvious without making the interface loud or frantic.
- Keep secondary actions discoverable, calm, and appropriately de-emphasized.
- Use structure and spacing to reduce cognitive load and guide attention intentionally.
- Design loading, empty, success, and error states with the same care as the happy path.
- Use copy, transitions, and feedback to reinforce understanding rather than add noise.
- Make system status, permissions, constraints, and consequences visible in the interface when they matter to user decisions.
- Design the interface so it reflects real underlying state rather than a stale, optimistic, or misleading approximation.
- Choose a visual direction on purpose instead of defaulting to generic, interchangeable UI patterns.
- Use motion to explain, orient, or reinforce behavior; keep it in service of comprehension.
- Make forms and workflows feel forgiving, guided, and easy to recover from.
- Preserve hierarchy and usability across mobile and desktop instead of merely shrinking layouts.
- Treat accessibility as a baseline quality bar: maintain contrast, focus visibility, touch target quality, keyboard support, and motion sensitivity awareness.
- Design for trust: users should not be surprised by hidden state, misleading affordances, or silent failure.
- Let ornament, tone, and delight support clarity and product identity rather than compete with them.

## Avoid

- Do not ship generic UI when the product needs a clearer or more intentional visual direction.
- Do not use motion, visual flourish, or novelty that weakens usability.
- Do not hide critical actions, states, or consequences behind subtle styling tricks.
- Do not show enabled actions, success cues, or reassuring UI states when the system cannot actually support them.
- Do not treat accessibility as optional polish.
- Do not allow responsive behavior to collapse hierarchy or break task flow.
